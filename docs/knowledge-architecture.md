# Knowledge Architecture

This document defines the authoritative architecture for Knowledge in ASCOS.

Knowledge Architecture establishes the authoritative architectural model for knowledge as a governed organizational asset. It specifies how knowledge is defined, classified, governed, related, evolved, and reused so that products generated by ASCOS remain consistent, maintainable, and governed. This document is architectural. It does not prescribe implementation methods, operating mechanisms, or delivery choices.

## 1. Purpose

Knowledge Architecture exists to ensure that organizational knowledge is treated as a governed architectural asset rather than an informal by-product of work. It provides the enterprise foundation for identifying what knowledge exists, how it is classified, how it is related to other architectural concepts, and how it is preserved and evolved over time.

This architecture is required because knowledge influences decisions, product direction, governance, reuse, and consistency across ASCOS. By governing knowledge independently from implementation choices, ASCOS can maintain clear meaning and alignment even as the enterprise evolves. Knowledge Architecture contributes to consistent product generation by making shared understanding explicit, reusable, and durable.

## 2. Core Principles

The Knowledge Architecture is founded on the following principles:

- Knowledge is reusable: knowledge is an enterprise asset that can support many products, teams, and decisions.
- Knowledge is governed: knowledge requires ownership, stewardship, review, and controlled evolution.
- Knowledge is organizational: knowledge exists at the enterprise level and shapes how the organization understands itself and its work.
- Knowledge is implementation independent: the architecture defines what knowledge is without prescribing how it is realized or employed.
- Knowledge is technology neutral: the architecture remains valid across changing tools, methods, and future operating models.
- Knowledge supports consistency: shared knowledge reduces ambiguity and improves alignment across products and decisions.
- Knowledge supports quality: governed knowledge improves judgment, review, and architectural soundness.
- Knowledge evolves through governance: change is managed through approved stewardship and review.
- Knowledge is versionable: understanding must remain traceable as it changes over time.
- Knowledge promotes reuse: reusable knowledge reduces duplication and preserves enterprise coherence.

## 3. Knowledge Definition

A Knowledge asset is structured organizational understanding that provides meaning, guidance, rationale, constraints, and context for performing activities and making business, engineering, and architectural decisions.

Knowledge is distinct from other architectural concepts:

- Data: observed facts or recorded signals that may be interpreted and contextualized.
- Information: organized data that has been interpreted for a specific purpose.
- Documentation: a record or representation of knowledge, but not knowledge itself.
- Code: a formal expression of instructions or logic, not the same as organizational understanding.
- Skills: abilities required to act, which depend on knowledge but are not equivalent to it.
- Tools: operational resources that support activity, not the understanding that governs it.
- Execution: the carrying out of work, which is distinct from the knowledge that informs and shapes it.

## 4. Architectural Distinctions

Knowledge Architecture preserves clear architectural distinctions between knowledge and related concepts:

- Capability: an organizational ability that may be informed by knowledge but is not itself knowledge.
- Role: an accountable responsibility pattern that may depend on knowledge but does not define it.
- Digital Twin: a fulfilment representation aligned to a Business Role, not a knowledge asset in itself.
- Decision: a deliberate choice that may be informed by knowledge but is not the same as knowledge.
- Skill: a reusable competence unit that depends on knowledge for effective application.
- Tool: an operational resource that supports the application of skills and decisions.

These distinctions preserve architectural clarity and avoid conflating governance, responsibility, and understanding.

## 5. Knowledge Classification

Knowledge Architecture uses a stable classification model to improve discovery, governance, and reuse.

Representative categories include:

- Business Knowledge
- Domain Knowledge
- Engineering Knowledge
- Governance Knowledge
- Security Knowledge
- Quality Knowledge
- Operational Knowledge
- Product Knowledge

These categories describe the purpose and architectural role of knowledge domains. They do not define organizational structure or operating roles.

## 6. Knowledge Structure

Knowledge should be understood as an organized architectural structure rather than an unbounded collection of statements.

Conceptually, knowledge may be organized through:

- Domains: broad areas of understanding that span the enterprise.
- Areas: focused subsets of knowledge within a domain.
- Components: meaningful units of knowledge with a defined boundary and purpose.
- Relationships: explicit links that show how knowledge components influence, depend on, or reinforce one another.

This structure supports clarity, reuse, review, and governance without prescribing any implementation form.

## 7. Knowledge Relationships

Knowledge Architecture defines how knowledge relates to other enterprise architectural concepts.

Knowledge may influence:

- Decisions: by providing rationale, constraints, and context.
- Skills: by informing expected competence and judgment.
- Tools: by shaping appropriate use and selection expectations.
- Roles: by clarifying responsibility-relevant understanding.
- Digital Twins: by informing how responsibilities are interpreted and fulfilled.
- Capabilities: by strengthening the quality and consistency of enterprise ability.

These relationships are architectural influences. They do not transfer authority, accountability, or operational responsibility.

## 8. Knowledge Composition

Larger bodies of organizational knowledge are composed from smaller knowledge components in a way that preserves consistency, governance, and reuse.

Composition should be intentional and traceable. Shared knowledge should be reused rather than redefined across products or domains. Where knowledge is grouped into larger bodies, the boundaries, dependencies, and governance expectations must remain explicit. Composition supports enterprise coherence without collapsing distinct knowledge assets into a single undifferentiated body.

## 9. Knowledge Context

Knowledge exists within organizational context and must be understood in relation to the environment in which it is used.

Relevant contexts include:

- Business Context: the commercial, strategic, and stakeholder environment that gives knowledge its purpose.
- Engineering Context: the engineering and architectural environment in which knowledge is interpreted and applied.
- Organizational Context: the governance, role, and accountability environment that determines ownership and use.
- Product Context: the product intent, scope, and outcomes that shape the relevance of knowledge.

Knowledge Architecture addresses these contexts at the architectural level and preserves them as enterprise framing for governance and interpretation.

## 10. Knowledge Lifecycle

Knowledge progresses through a conceptual lifecycle that preserves its integrity and usefulness over time.

The lifecycle includes:

- Creation: the initial recognition or development of a knowledge asset.
- Validation: the checking of clarity, quality, scope, and relevance.
- Approval: the formal recognition that the knowledge is suitable for enterprise use.
- Publication: the making available of approved knowledge for reuse and reference.
- Evolution: the refinement, correction, or extension of knowledge as context changes.
- Retirement: the controlled withdrawal of knowledge when it becomes obsolete or superseded.

This lifecycle is an architectural governance model rather than a prescribed operating sequence.

## 11. Knowledge Governance

Knowledge governance establishes the architectural responsibilities required to preserve knowledge integrity and reuse.

Governance includes:

- Ownership: clear accountability for the existence and quality of a knowledge asset.
- Stewardship: ongoing responsibility for maintenance, review, and coherence.
- Accountability: responsibility for consequences arising from the use or misapplication of knowledge.
- Review: periodic or event-driven examination of relevance, correctness, and alignment.
- Version Management: preserving the history and progression of knowledge assets over time.
- Change Approval: controlled authorization for significant updates or replacements.
- Consistency Assurance: ensuring that knowledge remains aligned to enterprise principles and related architectures.

## 12. Contribution to ASCOS Product Generation

Knowledge Architecture enables ASCOS to generate products with greater reuse, consistency, and quality.

It supports:

- Reuse: shared knowledge can be applied across multiple products and initiatives.
- Consistency: common understanding reduces divergence and rework.
- Decision quality: better knowledge improves judgment and rationale.
- Product quality: aligned and governed knowledge strengthens product decisions and outcomes.
- Organizational alignment: knowledge creates a shared basis for enterprise action and interpretation.
- Sustainable product generation: knowledge provides durable intellectual infrastructure for long-term product creation.

Knowledge Architecture contributes to sustainable product generation by making understanding explicit and governed rather than implicit and fragmented.

## 13. Architectural Boundaries

Knowledge Architecture governs the definition, classification, governance, relationships, lifecycle, and reuse of organizational knowledge.

It explicitly excludes:

- Runtime execution
- Memory systems
- AI reasoning
- Storage technologies
- Retrieval technologies
- User interaction
- Implementation technologies

Knowledge Architecture defines what knowledge is and how it should be governed. It does not define how knowledge is realized, processed, or executed.

## 14. Relationship to Other Architectures

Knowledge Architecture is complementary to the other ASCOS architecture domains.

- Constitution: provides the governing principles and normative expectations within which knowledge is interpreted.
- Company Design: establishes the organizational structure that gives knowledge its ownership and stewardship context.
- Engineering Capability Model: provides the capability landscape that knowledge helps to sustain and improve.
- Role Architecture: defines the responsibilities that rely on knowledge and its appropriate use.
- Digital Twin Architecture: frames how knowledge informs the fulfilment of Business Roles.
- Decision Architecture: governs how knowledge informs, supports, and preserves decisions.
- Skill Architecture: defines the competence that depends on knowledge for meaningful application.
- Tool Architecture: defines the operational resources that may support the use of knowledge in practice.

These architectures work together to maintain enterprise coherence without overlapping in purpose.

## 15. Extension Principles

Knowledge Architecture should be extended only in ways that preserve its architectural integrity.

Extensions must preserve:

- Consistency: new knowledge categories or relationships must remain coherent with the enterprise model.
- Governance: new knowledge assets must remain subject to ownership, stewardship, and review.
- Independence: extensions must remain architecture-level and not become implementation guidance.
- Reuse: new knowledge should be designed for reuse across contexts rather than isolated use.
- Architectural compatibility: extensions must remain compatible with the broader ASCOS architecture model.

Extensions strengthen the architecture when they increase clarity, governance, and reuse without weakening boundaries.

## 16. Design Principles Summary

Knowledge Architecture provides the enterprise foundation for governing organizational understanding across ASCOS. Its purpose is to define knowledge as a reusable, governed, organizational asset that supports consistency, quality, and long-term product generation. It establishes clear scope, classification, relationships, lifecycle, and governance expectations while remaining independent of implementation choices.

As the authoritative Knowledge Architecture specification for ASCOS, this document defines what knowledge is, how it is classified and related, how it evolves, and how it contributes to enterprise coherence, decision quality, and sustainable product generation.
